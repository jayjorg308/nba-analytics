# The verdict is authored prose, kept honest by a committed claims guard

The page's verdict — the plain-words answer to the thesis, rendered directly under the title — is **authored copy** in `heroConfig` (`verdict`), not a computed template. What keeps authored prose from lying is `src/app/verdict.guard.test.ts`: a committed test that maps each directional claim in the copy to an assertion against the **deployed payload's** computed metrics, plus a lexical tripwire for ADR-0005 creation vocabulary.

**Why:** a computed template ("selection is {band}, making is {direction}") was rejected because the verdict is the product's voice — fifteen words of editorial judgment ("the problem is making … almost entirely from three") that threshold-driven text can't write without sounding like a stats site, which is the exact thing the verdict exists to not be. Unguarded copy was rejected because it drifts silently: a `hero:sync` on a live season (or a hero swap) can invert a claim while the sentence keeps rendering. The guard is the same pattern as the ADR-0014 palette guard — values are free, invariants are enforced — applied to prose: copy is free, claims are enforced. Where a claim needs a semantic ("essentially league-average", "reads below league"), the guard reuses established semantics: the making scale's neutral band (ADR-0013) defines "reads at league average", and the combined-threes rollup (ADR-0016) supplies the grain where the three-point claim clears the small-sample bar.

**Consequences:** when the data moves, rewrite the copy *and* the guard's claim mapping together — never loosen an assertion to keep stale prose alive. The guard file is part of the hero copy: swapping heroes means a new verdict and a new claim mapping (the thresholds — the ±0.02 PPS league-average band, the 0.05 PPS materiality bar — are verdict semantics and live in the guard with their rationale). The verdict states no numerals; the headline blocks beneath carry the figures, so copy and formatted numbers cannot disagree about a digit.
